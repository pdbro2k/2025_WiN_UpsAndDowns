Moravian Memoirs

Moravian Memoirs

Introduction

Moravians

  • one of the oldest Protestant denominations
  • emerged from 15th century movements
  • "renewed" by Count von Zinzendorf in 1727

Moravian Memoirs

  • "semi-autobiographical" sources
    • based on autobiographical notes
    • composed by local church members
  • "obligatory" since 1747

Moravian Memoirs

Corpus

Sampling

  • Time frame: 1750-1850
  • Language: German
  • Datasources (89 memoirs):
    • Moravian Lives (Bucknell University)
    • Bethlehemer Schwestern (K. Faull)
    • Moravian Men’s Memoirs (K. Faull)
    • Bethlehem Digital History Project
    • > 300 more in the process of digitization
  • Metadata:
    • year/place of birth
    • year/place of death
    • gender

Distinct Features

  • emotionality (Van Gent, 2017; Faull and McGuire 2022)
  • mobility (due to missionary work)

    Moravian Lives

Moravian Memoirs

Structure

Memoirs typically [...] begin[...] with basic biographical information, birth, and early childhood experience, followed by a description of adolescence into adulthood, then marriage, occupation, and middle adulthood, followed lastly by later adulthood and death. For those who died young or unexpectedly, the final two sections may be different [...]. This final section was often written by the choir helper.
McGuire (2021, 132)

Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment Analysis

Definition

the computational study of people’s opinions, appraisals, attitudes, and emotions toward entities, individuals, issues, events, topics and their attributes

Liu and Zhang (2012, 415)

Types
  • Binary Classification: positive vs. negative
  • Ternary Classification: positive vs. neutral vs. negative
  • Quaternary Classification: positive vs. neutral vs. negative vs. mixed
  • Ordinal Classification: e.g. 1 to 5 stars
  • ...
Granularities
  • documents
  • sentences
  • spans (of fixed length)

Sentiment Analysis

Sentiment Trendlines | Expectations

one would expect [...] polarity to begin as neutral [...] as beginning content contains basic demographic facts. There should then be a rising to a general positive peak in early childhood [...]. The positive peak should then fall through adolescence and young adulthood [...] as people describe challenging experiences. Positivity should then rise slightly as people describe the conversion experience and joining the church [...]. As midlife and stability approaches, the polarity should decrease some from the conversion experience and testimonial section but remain positive overall. [...] positive polarity [...] should then rise sharply in a final peak as people describe finding peace with Christ and “going home”. Although illness generally also occurs before death and has some negative component, positive sentiment should predominate as Moravians viewed this trial and experience as bringing them closer to christ and a final positive outcome of the life journey.
McGuire (2021, 133)
